DUFFING OSCILLATOR

The Duffing oscillator is one of the prototype systems of nonlinear dynamics. It first became popular for studying anharmonic oscillations and, later, chaotic nonlinear dynamics in the wake of early studies by the engineer Georg Duffing.

The duffing system has been successfully used to model a variety of physical processes such as stiffening springs, beam buckling, nonlinear electronic circuits, superconducting Josephson parametric amplifiers, and ionization waves in plasmas.

Nowadays, the term "Duffing equation" is used for any equation that describes an oscillator that has a cubic stiffness term, regardless of the type of damping or excitation.

Chaotic Systems:

The main idea of chaos theory is that a minor difference at the start of a process can make a major change in it as time progresses. The dynamical systems of interest in chaos studies are nonlinear

For example, if you start with your stereo at low volume and turn the volume control one unit, the volume increases by one unit. If you now turn the control two units, the volume increases two units. This is an example of a linear response. In a nonlinear system, linear superposition fails and a system need not change proportionally to the change in a variable. If you turn your volume control too far, the volume may not only increase more than the number of units of the turn, but whistles and various other distortions occur in the sound. These are examples of a nonlinear response.

Increases the driving force will push the system from deterministic dynamics to chaotic dynamics that cannot be predicted exactly. Investigating the behaviour of the system as γ increases yields interesting results that are made most clear if we study both the beginning and ending behaviour of each solution obtained for various values of γ.



After changing the values of γ incrementally, we came to find that as γ increases more than 0.35, we see that the system has transitioned from the phase space where bifurcation occurs into a region of chaos. This does not allow for the system to eventually reach a stable, fixed behaviour but instead the solutions continue to move through the phase-space in an unpredictable fashion.

Thus, we can say that due to energy conservation, that the Duffing Equation is unable to exhibit chaos when the oscillator is undamped and unforced (that is, δ = γ = 0). To allow for chaos, energy conservation is eliminated by including both a damping and an external forcing term. Then we see, as the magnitude of external forcing is increased, the system moves through a region of period-doubling bifurcations and then transitions to a chaotic regime. The transition to chaos appears to occur around γ = 0.35
